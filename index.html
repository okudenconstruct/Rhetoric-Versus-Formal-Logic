🧠 Rhetoric vs. Logic: High‑Profile Conservative Reactions to Political Violence

Contrasting high‑profile conservative rhetoric on political violence with formal logic tests. Built for GitHub Pages—fully static, responsive, and print‑friendly.

🗣️ + 🧩 Claims & Logic (click to expand)

▶ A1. “Radical left rhetoric caused this violence.” 🎯 Post hoc🔁 Equivocation
  • Assertion: Speech temporally precedes violence, therefore speech caused it.
  • Logical issue: Correlation ≠ causation; ignores confounders and counterfactuals.
  • Consistency test: If rhetoric causes violence, evaluate right‑wing rhetoric by the same rule.
Show formal schema
Observed: R_L before V. Infer: R_L ⇒ V (sole cause). Missing: {controls, alternative causes}. If ∀x[S(x)→¬V], blaming speech contradicts premise.
▶ A2. “We have to beat the hell out of radical left lunatics.” ⚖️ Contradiction🔥 Incitement drift
  • Preaches law‑and‑order/nonviolence while using violent exhortations.
  • Signals acceptance of force as a political tool; escalates risk of copycat harm.
Show formal schema
Norm: Respond to speech with speech (¬Force). Utterance U recommends Force. Therefore U violates the professed norm.
▶ A3. “Those celebrating political violence should face consequences.” 🧭 Special pleading🛡️ Free‑speech carve‑out
  • Creates exceptions for offensive speech despite claiming universal protection.
  • Often implemented as doxing/retaliation, a coercive response to speech.
Show formal schema
∀x[S(x)→P(x)]. ∃y[S(y)∧Offends→Sanction(y)]. Hence ¬P(y) for disliked y — contradicts universality.
▶ A4. “We will punish those who contribute to ‘hostile climates.’” ⚠️ Vagueness⛔ Prior restraint
  • Undefined standard enables arbitrary enforcement against disfavored viewpoints.
  • Threat of punishment chills lawful speech.
Show formal schema
Rule R lacks determinate conditions → cannot be applied consistently; chilling effect ⇒ practical suppression of protected speech.
▶ A5. “This proves the Left is the real threat.” 👥 Composition📊 Hasty generalization
  • Attributes actions of some individuals to an entire group identity.
  • Ignores counter‑examples and base rates of violence across groups.
Show formal schema
∃x[x∈L ∧ Violent(x)] ⟹ ∀y[y∈L→Threat(y)]  // invalid property lift from part→whole.
▶ A6. “Universities encourage violence by allowing left‑wing speakers.” 🧪 False cause🎓 Policy non sequitur
  • Permitting speech ≠ endorsing outcomes; conflates platforming with causation.
  • Leads to censorship proposals that undercut academic freedom.
Show formal schema
Permit(U, speech_z) ∧ Controversial(z) ⇏ Cause(U, violence). Relationship is non‑causal.
▶ A7. “Either you’re with us or you support political violence.” 🧩 False dilemma
  • Erases middle positions (e.g., condemn violence + defend civil liberties).
  • Polarizes debate to pre‑justify retaliation.
Show formal schema
Only {Us, Pro‑violence} presented as exhaustive. But ∃ alternatives A,B,… ⇒ dichotomy is invalid.
▶ A8. “The media has blood on its hands.” 🧷 Guilt by association📦 Burden shift
  • Asserts institutional culpability without specific causal chain.
  • Demands critics disprove responsibility rather than providing evidence.
Show formal schema
¬∃evidence(Media cause V) but claim insists on culpability; shifts proof burden: Opponent must refute undefined link.
▶ A9. “Anyone condemning conservatives is complicit in violence.” 🧷 Guilt by association🗨️ Speech‑punishment conflation
  • Conflates offensive speech with moral liability for others’ acts.
  • Creates collective punishment logic that undermines free‑speech norms.
Show formal schema
S(critique) ∧ Agent commits V ⇏ Complicit(critic). Liability requires intent/causal contribution, not mere opinion.
▶ A10. “Real conservatives would never do this — so the right isn’t to blame.” 🏴 No True Scotsman
  • Redefines the group after counter‑examples appear to protect the thesis.
Show formal schema
∃x[x∈C ∧ Violent(x)]. Redefine C’ := {y|y∈C ∧ ¬Violent(y)} ⇒ claim becomes unfalsifiable.

📝 Summary

Prominent reactions frame political violence as proof of a uniquely Left‑wing threat. They pledge allegiance to universal free speech while calling for punishment of offensive speech, denounce violence while using violent metaphors, and claim moral leadership while applying standards only to opponents.

⚔️ One‑Line Rebuttals

🧭 If speech isn’t violence, you can’t blame opponents’ speech for violence.
🛡️ Punishing offensive speech abandons free‑speech principles.
⚖️ Violent metaphors contradict claimed nonviolence.
👥 Some celebrants ≠ an entire political identity (composition fallacy).
🔁 “Whataboutism” doesn’t answer the charge; it concedes inconsistency.
🧪 Post hoc isn’t proof — correlation ≠ causation.
🎯 Address the claim, not the claimant (no ad hominem).
🧩 False dilemmas hide better options; real policy isn’t binary.
🧱 Redefining “true conservatives” to dodge counter‑examples = No True Scotsman.
